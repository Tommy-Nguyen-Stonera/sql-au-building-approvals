Project Overview & Motivation

Research Question
Do the materials and finishes I sell every day actually drive measurable differences in property value?
analysis.py

Conversations with homeowners almost always circle back to the same question: "Will this actually increase what my home is worth?" I have answered that hundreds of times based on experience and industry knowledge, but I had never tested those instincts against hard data. This project let me do exactly that.

The goal was not academic. I wanted something I could reference in real conversations with clients and contractors. When someone asks whether upgrading from vinyl siding to cement board is worth the cost, I want an answer backed by data, not just a hunch.

Where I Got Stuck Early

My first attempt used pd.read_csv() without specifying sep="\t" — the file was tab-separated, not comma-separated. Pandas silently loaded the entire row as a single column instead of throwing an error. I had a DataFrame with 2,930 rows and 1 column, and it took me embarrassingly long to realise what happened. The fix was trivial (sep="\t"), but it is a reminder that Pandas will happily give me a "valid" result that is completely wrong. I always check df.shape and df.head() immediately after loading.

Research Question
Which exterior cladding materials are associated with the highest property values? If a homeowner chooses between vinyl, brick, or cement board, what does the data say?

I initially plotted all exterior materials separately, but some (like stone and asbestos shingle) had fewer than 10 observations. The box plots for those materials were visually dramatic but statistically meaningless — one outlier could swing the median by $50K. I set a threshold of 30 observations and grouped everything below that into "Other." This is a trade-off: I lose the ability to comment on rare materials, but I gain confidence that the patterns I report are real.

Cement board (fibre cement) tops the list at $238K median — $35K above vinyl siding and $103K above metal siding. Business Implication

The $35K gap between cement board and vinyl gives a concrete answer to the upgrade question. The material cost difference is a fraction of that gap. For any homeowner weighing the upgrade, the data supports going premium on exterior cladding — it is one of the more accessible ways to associate a property with higher value.

Overall Quality Rating & Price

Research Question
How much does overall build quality actually matter? Is the relationship linear, or do the top tiers command a disproportionate premium?

# Median price per quality level
qual_med = df.groupby("Overall Qual")["SalePrice"].median()
# Quality 5 to 7: +$67,000
# Quality 7 to 9: +$160,000 — it accelerates!

Each quality point adds roughly $40,000 to median sale price, but the relationship is not linear — it accelerates at the top end. Quality 5 to 7 adds $67K, but quality 7 to 9 adds $160K. That acceleration is the most important finding in this entire analysis.

Key Finding

Overall quality has the strongest correlation with sale price in the entire dataset (r = 0.80). The jump from "good" to "excellent" is worth more than double the jump from "below average" to "good." For anyone specifying finishes, this is the argument for going premium on details that drive quality perception: cabinetry, trim, hardware, stone benchtops.

What I Expected vs What I Found

I assumed diminishing returns at the top end — that going from quality 8 to 9 would add less than going from 5 to 6. The opposite is true. Premium finishes compound in value. The market pays disproportionately more for excellence — which challenges the conventional wisdom I hear from builders every week: "Don't over-capitalise." The data suggests what builders call over-capitalisation is not over-capitalisation at all, at least at the premium end.

The Pivot Moment

I started this analysis expecting exterior cladding to be the strongest material-related predictor — because that is what I sell. But kitchen quality beats every individual exterior material comparison. The interior finish quality drives more value than the facade. This shifted my entire framing: I went from "which material matters most?" to "how well should I build, regardless of material?" The quality of execution matters more than the specific product. From my years in building materials, this resonates — the best tile installation with mid-range tiles looks better than a poor installation with premium tiles.

Where I Got Stuck

This finding made me cautious about every other comparison. If foundation type is really just a proxy for overall build quality, then how many of my other material comparisons are also proxies? The correlation heatmap was partly motivated by this question — I wanted to see which features had independent predictive power versus which were just riding along with overall quality. From my years in building materials, I know that foundation choices are made by builders, not homeowners — they are a build-quality signal, not a consumer preference signal. That distinction matters for how I interpret every chart in this report.

Key Finding

Quality ratings consistently outperform raw square footage in predicting price. The top five correlates include four quality measures and only one area measure. How I build matters as much as how big I build. Overall Quality dominates at r = 0.80 — nothing else comes close.

Where I Got Stuck

The ordinal quality columns (Kitchen Qual, Exter Qual, etc.) were stored as strings like "Ex", "Gd", "TA", "Fa", "Po." Pandas rightly refused to compute correlations on string columns. I had to create a mapping dictionary (qual_map) and transform each one to numeric. The frustrating part was that Fireplace Qu and Bsmt Qual had NaN values for properties without fireplaces or basements. The .map() call silently returned NaN for those, which then caused the correlation calculation to drop those rows. I initially did not notice this and was getting slightly different correlation values depending on which features I included. The fix was understanding that Pandas drops NaN rows pairwise in correlation by default — so each correlation pair might use a different subset of rows. This is correct behaviour, but it tripped me up.

Surprises, Self-Critique & Next Steps

Research Question
What surprised me, where did my industry assumptions break down, and what would I investigate next?
Retrospective

Heating quality (r = 0.50) was surprisingly relevant. I would not have guessed that HVAC quality correlates as strongly as masonry veneer area. It raises the question of whether buyers are explicitly evaluating HVAC systems during inspections, or whether heating quality is another proxy for overall build standard. If it is the former, there is a marketing angle for HVAC suppliers that I had not considered.

The quality curve accelerates, it does not flatten. This was the most consequential surprise. The conventional wisdom in the trades is "don't over-capitalise" — meaning there is a ceiling above which spending more does not increase property value proportionally. The Ames data says the opposite: the premium end compounds. The marginal cost of upgrading finishes from quality 7 to quality 9 is far less than the $160K in additional property value associated with it.

Honest Self-Critique

Looking back, I should have done three things differently. First, this analysis is mostly bivariate — I compare one feature against price at a time. Building a multivariate regression model with interaction terms would let me isolate the independent contribution of each material and quality feature, controlling for confounders like lot size and location. Second, this dataset is from Ames, Iowa. I work in Sydney. Running the same analysis on Australian property data would test whether these patterns hold across markets. Third, pairing this data with material cost data would let me calculate actual ROI for each upgrade. Stone veneer is associated with $102K more in price, but what does it cost to install? That cost-to-value ratio is what clients actually need.

The Core Takeaway

If I had to distil this entire analysis into one sentence for a client conversation, it would be this: How I build matters as much as how big I build. Quality of execution — materials, finishes, attention to detail — correlates with property value as strongly as square footage. That is the most useful thing I have learned from this data, and it aligns with what I have seen in five years of selling premium building materials.
